About Me

I am curious about the influence of materiality in technology. Analog and digital objects alike rely on their visual, physical, and social design for intuitive interaction. But with the rapid increase in automated, agentic, and responsive objects — how do these markers of interaction change? My work explores unconventional, overlooked, and unfamiliar presentations of objects to prod at these boundaries.

Robot Fashion

Overview

Experimental 3D printed fashion accessories designed to protect humanoid limbs from damage when falling. Resulted from previous work on 3D printing soft skin for the HIRO group's Franka robot. Using Blender + GenTact (a Blender extension made by lab member Carson Kholbrenner) I created TPU (thermoplastic polyurethane) accessories that utilized the material's soft, bouncy properties to protect the robot's limbs from excessive force when falling.

Collaboration with HIRO group at CU Boulder.

Skills

3D printing, CAD design, TPU printing, multimaterial printing

Topics

Human-Robot-Interaction, 3D printing, tactile interaction design, fashion

More Info

After joining the HIRO lab, I began working with PhD student Carson Kholbrenner on his soft robotic skin project, where I learned how to use Blender and Carson's extension GenTact Toolbox to design skins directly on top of the robot's morphology. Once I was familiar with the routine CAD-to-3D printing pipeline, I began exploring various filaments such as Verashore (a filament that foams at high temperatures) and TPU (a soft, bouncy plastic) to expand sensory interactions between person and robot. I settled on TPU, given its springier properties and ability to contract under pressure and absorb excess force.

Before designing fashion accessories, I was asked to experiment with creating a dual-layer skin using both PLA and TPU to mimic the way our own human skin changes color when gently pressed. I turned to cosplay and diorama building communities for inspiration, particularly interested in the ways they manipulate textures of plastic and foam to evoke other materials. I designed a blunted spiked PLA underlayer, which would press up against a softer upper layer. When colliding with an object this second layer would press down into the blunted PLA spikes, and the tops of the spikes would be seen and registered by an external camera, communicating pressure applied to the skin!

We ultimately decided on using TPU since it provided an easier, one-time printing process. Incorporating conductive PLA in our design to embed tactile sensing into the skin itself turned this into a multi-material printing project! Using the lab's Prusa XL I learned how to properly prepare, troubleshoot, and print with multiple materials.

The resulting skins were made entirely of TPU and conductive PLA. They were flattened from their original hoop-like structure to limit unexpectedly catching on surrounding objects and adhered to the humanoid's limbs avoiding joints and being mindful of configuration spaces for each limb.

BioTelia

Overview

Interactive installation using fabric, motion capture, and insect-like robots. Positioning the audience as a pollinator in our ecosystem, BioTelia combines robotics, materiality, and performance technology to create an alien environment that echoes systems of our own world. Each 'pollinator' was tracked by a colorful light that changed when subjects came in contact with the larger tree-like structures. I was in charge of designing the robots, which acted as secondary pollinators next to the humans. Each robot had different interaction properties — resulting in different reactions and projected personalities from the audience!

Collaboration with Shalimar Alvarado Cruz, Aaron Neyer, Cambria Klinger, Ayesha Rawal for ATLAS design studio course.

Code of Conduct

Overview

Typeface created from 2000+ handwriting samples collected across CU Boulder whiteboards. Over three weeks I routinely visited campus whiteboards across the library, classrooms, communal study spaces in disciplines ranging from engineering, English, chemistry, and art. I photographed every board and isolated each letter in the image using MAXQDA, a qualitative coding platform. Then, I digitized each letterform in Adobe Photoshop and assembled them all into a typeface using Glyphs Mini. The resulting typeface is a record of CU Boulder student handwriting, and the type specimen re-imagines public school signage in their voice.

Skills

Data collection and analysis, digital typography design. Tools: Adobe, Glyphs, MAXQDA

Topics

Handwriting, community spaces, data visualization

AI Puppet

Overview

Puppet to educate children on AI literacy through creating a personalized agent. Demonstrated at ATLAS Open Labs, participants could create their own 'puppet' by describing personality attributes, speech restrictions, and drawing out their puppet on a sticky note. The sticky note was then placed beside the screen, so participants could look at it while verbally conversing with their constructed AI agent. This project is still in progress and positioned to collaborate with local Boulder schools to teach students about the limitations, hallucinations, and abilities of AI agents to promote AI literacy.

Collaboration with ACME lab members Krithik Ranjan and Suibi Che-Chuan Weng.

Skills

Qualitative analysis, coding

Topics
